# Rewrite Guide — the antidote

How to fix sloppy writing, not just flag it. Removing AI tells is only half the
job; the other half is putting *substance* back in. This is the target the
rewrite aims at.

## The four antidote qualities

Across all five slop types, the cure is the same. Writing that has **any** of
these is almost certainly not slop:

1. **Specificity** — named people, named places, specific dates, exact numbers.
   *If you can fact-check it, it's probably not slop.*
2. **Lived experience** — personal stakes, specific failures, lessons that could
   only come from doing the thing, not from summarizing it.
3. **Cited sources** — named studies, linked references, attributed quotes. Every
   factual claim should be traceable to its origin.
4. **Information gain** — after reading, the reader knows something they didn't
   before. *Simplest test: can you summarize what was new?*

## Core rewrite principles

1. **Specificity over vagueness.** Replace generic phrasing with concrete
   details that couldn't apply to multiple subjects.
2. **Show your work.** Explain the HOW, not just the WHAT — timeframes,
   quantities, specific actions, lessons readers can actually implement.
3. **Take a stance.** Commit to a defendable opinion instead of safe platitudes.
   *Content that tries to please everyone pleases no one.*
4. **Include lived experience.** Surface failures, surprises, hard-won
   discoveries. *Failure stories > success stories.*

## Two tests to apply while rewriting

- **The Specificity Test:** Read a sentence. Could you swap in a completely
  different subject and it would still make sense? If yes, it's too vague.
- **The Deletion Test:** If you can delete a sentence (or the whole intro) and
  the paragraph still makes sense, delete it.

## Phrases to delete on sight

- Openers: "In today's fast-paced world…", "It's no secret that…", "In the
  ever-evolving landscape…"
- Fillers: "It's important to note that…", "At the end of the day…", "When all
  is said and done…"

## Anti-slop checklist (run before returning the rewrite)

- Does every paragraph contain at least one specific detail?
- Can you delete the intro paragraph?
- Does the content take a disagreeable / defendable stance?
- Does it include personal experience or concrete examples?
- Would you actually send this to a friend asking for help on this topic?

## The honesty constraint

The antidote calls for specifics, sources, and lived experience — but you are
editing someone else's text and **you do not have their facts.** Never fabricate
names, numbers, citations, or personal anecdotes to satisfy the checklist.

When a piece is empty of substance, the right move is to:
1. Tighten and de-slop what's there (cut filler, fix the tells), and
2. **Point out exactly where specifics, a source, or a real example are needed**
   and ask the author to supply them.

A shorter, honest rewrite that flags its gaps beats a longer one stuffed with
invented detail.
